Spring Studios, Tribeca

The epicenter of NYFW street style. Varick Street and surrounding blocks transform into a runway every morning between 8-10 AM and again at 5-7 PM. Highest concentration of photographers.

The Shed, Hudson Yards

New hotspot since 2024. The elevated walkway connecting The High Line to The Shed creates a natural corridor for street style. Best shooting angles at the 30th Street entrance.

Mercer Street, SoHo

The cobblestone blocks between Houston and Broome remain the gold standard for off-schedule street style. Fashion editors, stylists, and showroom visitors create a constant stream of looks.

Dimes Square, LES

The unofficial after-hours zone. Canal and Division Streets draw the downtown crowd between shows. Edgier, more experimental looks dominate here, especially after 8 PM.

Industry City, Brooklyn

Brooklyn's answer to Manhattan fashion week. The waterfront walkway and inner courtyards attract a distinctly different crowd: independent designers, artists, and sustainable fashion advocates.

Cipriani Corridor, FiDi

Wall Street transforms during evening shows. The intersection of Broad and Wall becomes a destination for formal fashion week looks and dramatic pre-show arrivals.

The Origin Story

From Press Week to Global Phenomenon

New York Fashion Week traces its origins to 1943, when publicist Eleanor Lambert organized the first-ever "Press Week" to draw attention away from French fashion during World War II. What began as a strategic publicity effort in a modest hotel ballroom evolved into the most commercially significant fashion event on Earth.

From the tents at Bryant Park to Lincoln Center, from the decentralized shows across downtown Manhattan to the digital pivot of 2020, NYFW has continuously reinvented itself. Today it generates an estimated $900 million in economic impact for New York City each season and serves as the launchpad for designers who go on to define global fashion.

Unlike Paris (haute couture), Milan (luxury goods), or London (avant-garde), New York Fashion Week has always been defined by its commercial energy, democratic spirit, and cultural diversity — a direct reflection of the city itself.

Featured Venue

Spring Studios, Tribeca

Since becoming NYFW's primary hub in 2015, Spring Studios at 50 Varick Street has hosted some of the most memorable runway moments of the past decade. Its four floors offer 60,000 square feet of flexible event space, from the intimate Studio 2 (capacity 200) to the expansive Main Gallery (capacity 800).

The building's raw concrete columns, exposed brick, and industrial skylights have become as iconic as the collections shown within them. Designers from Tom Ford and Coach to Proenza Schouler and LaQuan Smith have staged career-defining shows here, attracted by the venue's combination of downtown grit and uptown production capability.

Located at the crossroads of Tribeca and Hudson Square, Spring Studios also benefits from some of the best street style photography conditions in the city — wide cobblestone sidewalks, consistent natural light, and the kind of architectural backdrop that makes every outfit look editorial.

Questions & Answers

NYFW FAQ

New York Fashion Week takes place twice a year: the Spring/Summer collections are shown in September, and the Fall/Winter collections are shown in February. The next NYFW is scheduled for September 5–10, 2026, featuring over 180 shows across 24 venues in Manhattan and Brooklyn. The official calendar is managed by the CFDA (Council of Fashion Designers of America) and released approximately six weeks before the start of each fashion week.

NYFW shows are primarily invitation-only events for buyers, press, celebrities, and industry professionals. However, there are several paths to access: press accreditation through the CFDA for working journalists and editors; buyer registration for retail professionals and wholesale buyers; public-access events such as the NYFW: The Shows platform, which offers ticketed experiences and livestreaming; and emerging designer showcases and off-calendar events, which frequently welcome RSVPs from fashion students and enthusiasts. Our Show Access resource provides detailed guidance for every access level.

NYFW venues span across Manhattan and Brooklyn. The current primary hub is Spring Studios at 50 Varick Street in Tribeca. Other major venues include The Shed at Hudson Yards, Skylight Clarkson Square in the West Village, Cipriani Wall Street in the Financial District, the Angel Orensanz Foundation on the Lower East Side, Pier 17 at the South Street Seaport, and various galleries in Chelsea. Brooklyn venues, particularly Industry City in Sunset Park, have become increasingly important for off-calendar and independent designer shows. The full venue index with maps, transit directions, and capacity information is available in our Venue Index tab above.

On-calendar shows are officially sanctioned by the CFDA and appear on the official NYFW schedule. These shows benefit from centralized press coordination, buyer services, and the marketing weight of the NYFW brand. Off-calendar shows are independently produced by designers who choose to show during fashion week without CFDA affiliation. Both are considered part of the broader NYFW ecosystem, and some of the season's most talked-about presentations happen off-calendar. In recent years, the distinction has become less rigid, with major designers occasionally choosing off-calendar presentations for creative freedom, while the CFDA has expanded its calendar to include more emerging and independent voices.
