12 Categories Mapped

The Ecosystem Behind the Runway

Every collection that walks the runway depends on an invisible network of specialists, craftspeople, and service providers. These are the categories we document — the real infrastructure of fashion.

✂

Sample Rooms

Where collections are born. Sample rooms transform a designer's sketch into the first physical garment — the prototype that will be refined, fitted, and eventually replicated for production. Our directory covers 85+ sample rooms across the Garment District, Brooklyn, and Long Island City, with details on specialization, minimum order quantities, and turnaround times.

✍

Pattern Makers

The technical translators of fashion. Pattern makers take a designer's vision and create the precise templates that guide fabric cutting and construction. From draping specialists to digital CAD experts, our directory lists 60+ pattern makers in Manhattan and Brooklyn, including those who specialize in knitwear, tailoring, and avant-garde construction.

👕

Tailors

The artisans of fit and finish. From bespoke suiting to alterations for editorial shoots, New York's tailoring tradition spans generations and cultures. Our map includes 70+ tailors — Italian-trained master tailors on the Upper East Side, Savile Row alumni in Midtown, West African specialists in Harlem, and Chinese tailoring houses in Chinatown.

🎨

Fabric Stores

The raw material of design. New York's fabric ecosystem ranges from Mood Fabrics on West 37th Street (made famous by Project Runway) to hidden wholesale dealers in Chinatown, to specialty linen importers and deadstock fabric dealers in Brooklyn. We map 95+ fabric sources with details on material type, price range, and designer clientele.

🏢

Showrooms

Where collections meet buyers. Showrooms are the commercial nerve center of fashion — the physical spaces where designers present their lines to retailers, editors, and stylists. Our directory covers 80+ showrooms in SoHo, NoLita, Chelsea, and the Garment District, organized by brand tier, category, and representation model.

📣

PR Firms

The amplifiers. Fashion PR firms manage press outreach, celebrity dressing, influencer partnerships, and event production. From powerhouses like KCD and Bureau Betak to boutique agencies serving emerging designers, we track 55+ firms with details on specialization, client roster size, and service tiers. Essential reading for any designer seeking representation.

📷

Photographers

The image-makers. Fashion photography in New York spans editorial, lookbook, campaign, e-commerce, and street style. Our directory includes 65+ photographers and photography studios across the city, with details on style, rate ranges, studio space availability, and portfolio links. From Tribeca loft studios to Bushwick industrial spaces.

🎭

Casting

The faces of fashion. Casting directors and model agencies determine who walks the runway and who appears in campaigns. Our map includes 40+ casting agencies, model management firms, and talent scouts across Midtown, Chelsea, and Williamsburg — with data on diversity metrics, new face programs, and booking processes.

🎓

Fashion Schools

Where the next generation begins. From FIT on Seventh Avenue to Parsons in Greenwich Village to Pratt in Brooklyn, New York is home to the most influential fashion education institutions in the world. Our directory covers 15+ programs with details on curriculum, tuition, notable alumni, industry partnerships, and scholarship opportunities.

💡

Incubators

Launchpads for emerging talent. Fashion incubators provide shared studio space, mentorship, business education, and production resources to designers in their first years. We map 12+ incubator programs across the city, including the CFDA Fashion Incubator, the Brooklyn Fashion + Design Accelerator, and Manufacture New York.

🏭

Studios

Where the work happens. Design studios, shared ateliers, and co-working spaces built specifically for fashion professionals. From climate-controlled archival spaces to open-plan cutting rooms with natural light, our directory lists 50+ studio spaces with details on square footage, equipment, lease terms, and community features. Essential for designers seeking workspace.

🧳

Leather Suppliers

The material of luxury. New York's leather supply chain runs from the tanneries of the Hudson Valley to the wholesale dealers on West 30th Street. Our directory covers 25+ leather suppliers and exotic skin dealers, including specialists in vegetable-tanned hides, vegan alternatives, patent leather, suede, and embossed finishes used by handbag and shoe designers citywide.

The Heart of American Fashion

The Garment District: An Empire in 20 Blocks

Between 34th and 42nd Street, from Fifth Avenue to Ninth Avenue, lies the single most important concentration of fashion manufacturing infrastructure in the Western Hemisphere. At its peak in the 1950s, over 300,000 workers filled these blocks — cutters, sewers, pattern makers, pressers, and finishers producing the garments that defined American style.

Today, that number has fallen below 5,000. But what remains is irreplaceable. The sample rooms, trimming stores, button dealers, zipper specialists, and fabric wholesalers that survive in the Garment District form a production ecosystem that cannot be replicated anywhere else in the country. When a designer needs a prototype built in 48 hours, there is only one place to go.

NYRunway documents every surviving business in this district — their specialties, their histories, their capacity, and their fight for survival against rezoning pressures and rising rents. This is not nostalgia. This is a living map of an endangered ecosystem.

About the Industry Map

The NYRunway Industry Map is a comprehensive, interactive directory of New York City's fashion infrastructure. It catalogs over 750 locations across 12 categories — including sample rooms, pattern makers, tailors, fabric stores, showrooms, PR firms, photographers, casting agencies, fashion schools, incubators, studios, and leather suppliers. Think of it as the Google Maps plus LinkedIn of the New York fashion industry: a searchable, filterable, neighborhood-by-neighborhood map of the ecosystem that makes collections possible. Every listing includes details on specialization, capacity, price tier, and verified contact information.

Unlike a generic business directory such as Yelp or Google Maps, the NYRunway Industry Map is built specifically for fashion professionals and researchers. Every listing is categorized by fashion-specific criteria: type of production capability, minimum order quantities, turnaround times, material specializations, brand clientele tier, and price range. We also provide editorial context — historical background on each neighborhood's role in the fashion ecosystem, trend data on which areas are growing or declining, and investigative reporting on the economic forces reshaping the industry. This is a living document, not a static listing.

The Industry Map serves multiple audiences. Emerging designers use it to find sample rooms, pattern makers, and fabric suppliers when building their first collections. Established brands use it to identify new production partners and alternative suppliers. Fashion students and educators use it as a curriculum resource to understand the full scope of the industry beyond the runway. Journalists and researchers use it to investigate the economics of fashion production in New York. Urban planners and policy makers use it to understand the economic impact of the Garment District and advocate for its preservation. If you are connected to New York fashion in any capacity, this map is built for you.

The Industry Map is a living document that is updated on a rolling basis. Our research team conducts quarterly audits of every listed business to verify that locations are still operational, contact information is accurate, and service offerings have not changed. New businesses are added as they open, and closed businesses are archived with historical context rather than simply deleted — because understanding what has been lost is as important as knowing what remains. Major updates are published at the start of each fashion season, with supplementary updates following NYFW each February and September.
